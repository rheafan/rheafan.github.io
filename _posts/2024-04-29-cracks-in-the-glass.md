title: "Cracks in the Glass"

“So, how’s high school?”

“It’s okay.”

“Is there anything you like?”

“I like studying.”

“For what? Why?”

“Tests.”

Tests?

Chewing Ruby’s birthday cake, you glance up at me from the screen of your phone, so briefly I could’ve imagined it. It’s the first time I’ve seen you in three years, in a post-lockdown, post-pandemic Shanghai.

“Jason, put it down. Eat,” your mother says flatly, her face blank. You ignore her and keep scrolling underneath the table.

“Mommy, can I have another slice of my cake?” says your sister, tugging at your mom’s sleeve.  

She turns around, her previously frozen-over features suddenly relaxing and melting into affection as soon as her eyes fall upon her daughter. “Of course, honey, it’s your birthday,” she says, her voice sugared. “Take the biggest slice! Do you want the unicorn?” On top of her three-tiered cake is a beautiful glass unicorn, pink-tinted and perfect, rearing up on its hind legs.

Ruby fusses over her slice, picks out the fancy chocolate decorations and her precious unicorn, then pushes the rest of the stripped-bare cake to her dad without another glance. She starts playing with the glass unicorn, wiping the frosting off its hooves, checking it for cracks and smudges. Ruby is surrounded by colorfully wrapped presents, your parents flitting around her like busy bees attending to their queen, her elaborate pink cake a centerpiece on the dinner table. 

You sit alone, chair pushed away from the table, your back hunched over a glowing white screen, barely taking a bite of the food getting cold on your plate. Your parents occasionally chide you to put down your phone; every once in a while, the grandparents tell you to eat something, but no one really talks to you.

Three years ago, we were at the same restaurant, in a bao jian (a private room for big parties of people) filled with round tables, the kind with a layer of revolving glass on top. We were sitting next to each other - shoveling food, talking, doing anything together. Once, you were pretending to be asleep, lying on a row of tall-backed chairs that I pushed together, only to actually fall asleep, exhausted from swimming with me in the hotel pool earlier - you had finished your summer homework for the day, and even though you were already tired, you said you weren’t and swam with me anyway. You rolled off the chairs on accident, and for a single moment, you just sat there, too stunned to speak. I remember sitting there, shocked, frozen in place just like you were, scared that you were hurt. Then you threw your head back and burst out laughing, and I laughed right along with you. We laughed like we did when my mom brought us to the Shanghai World Financial Center, standing on a floor made of glass. Only now do I realize how that floor has fallen through.

We are a hundred stories off the ground, and you are pointing down at Shanghai’s landmarks for me, your American-born cousin. “Look! That’s the Oriental Pearl Tower. It lights up pink at night!” you shout gleefully, practically buzzing with excitement. I trace the direction of your finger to the distinct shape of the tower, two spheres spaced apart on a skinny metal building, towering above the rest of the skyline. You are scared yet fearless, in the way only a child can be. You jump up and down as hard as you can, mock-gasping in shock and pretending to spot a crack in the glass. “Are you scared?” you ask when you see me flinch. I shake my head no, trying to not seem so little-kid to my older cousin, but you see right through me. “Why would you be? It’s just glass!” You laugh as I hesitate to follow in your steps, tiptoeing as though the glass was paper-thin. “Scaredy-cat,” you say jokingly, hopping on one foot and pretending to fall. 

Your eyes are bright and expectant as we lie on the floor, peering down at the city spread out underneath us. “Look at what’s under the glass!” you say. “The cars are small as ants! Look, that’s the highway my dad takes to work! Do you think we can spot him?” Hands pressed to the glass, we look down at the wide road that snakes through Shanghai, watching the tiny colorful cars slowly inch their way across the city. The layer of glass between you and the world was only so thick. Anything must have seemed possible to us then - us kids. 

Since when was our family WeChat group filled only with photos of Ruby’s piano recitals, swimming competitions, and report cards? You stopped popping up and stopped saying hi in my dad’s WeChat calls to his brother, you stopped taking our grandpa’s phone and spamming the family group chat with stickers and memes. Where were you? More importantly, how were you? Miles across the ocean, the one tiny window I had into your life was when my mom tutored you English over video call. I remember seeing your eyes flit from the computer camera down to what anyone could assume was your phone, hearing your vague answers that eventually frustrated my mom enough to stop the weekly sessions. I knew you were a lot different from the carefree, bubbly kid I used to run around Shanghai with - so when I heard my parents finally planning for a summer trip back to China, I tried to imagine what you’d be like now.

Nothing could have prepared me for the shock. Gone was the bright-eyed, bushy-tailed boy I knew, replaced by a moody, closed-off teenager. I sat quietly at the dinner table, hands folded in my lap, trying not to stare. At the very end of our family reunion dinner, as the adults said their goodbyes, the room suddenly felt very small and stuffy. I went outside and began wandering aimlessly as you leaned against the wall, vacant eyes glued to the screen of your phone. Trying to break the ice I didn’t even know was there, I peered over at your game and started asking random questions about it. I was trying to play the role of the annoying little cousin, a role that used to come so naturally to me it didn’t even need to be played. You shrugged me off and looked back down at your phone. My mind was spinning - what should I ask about? School? No, no, your family must ask enough about school already. Friends? According to my aunt and uncle, you don’t really have any - they called you a zhai nan, a homebody. Unsure of what to say next, I just started babbling about that silly monster game you used to love so much when you were little. What I really wanted was to ask how you were.

A simple question: three words, nine letters. How are you? Just an opening phrase, the start of a conversation between two cousins who hadn’t seen each other for five years, catching up with each other’s lives. I didn’t really expect an honest answer to it, but I couldn’t bring myself to ask anyway. Was it always this hard to talk to you? No. No, it definitely wasn’t.
